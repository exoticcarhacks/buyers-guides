# BMW M3 M4 F8X Generation 2015-2020 (3.0L twin-turbo I6, includes CS) Buyer Guide

## Introduction
The BMW F8X generation M3 and M4 represent a significant departure from their naturally aspirated predecessors, embracing a potent 3.0L twin-turbocharged inline-six engine. This era of M cars offers a compelling blend of high performance, daily usability, and modern technology, making them prime candidates for the Exotic Car Hacks philosophy. The primary risk associated with these vehicles lies in the potential for significant repair costs if common issues are not addressed proactively or if the car has been poorly maintained.

## Powertrain

The F8X M3 and M4 are powered by BMW's S55 3.0L twin-turbocharged inline-six engine, producing **425 hp (431 PS)** and **406 lb-ft of torque** in standard form. The Competition Package boosts output to **444 hp (450 PS)**. Power is sent to the rear wheels via either a 6-speed manual transmission or a 7-speed M Double-Clutch Transmission (M-DCT) with Drivelogic.

**Performance Figures:**
*   **0-60 mph:**
    *   Standard models: 4.1 seconds (6-speed manual), 3.9 seconds (M-DCT)
    *   Competition Package models: 3.8 seconds (M-DCT)
*   **Top Speed:** Electronically limited to 155 mph (250 km/h), or 174 mph (280 km/h) with the optional M Driver's Package.

## Known Problems and Recalls

A thorough Pre-Purchase Inspection (PPI) by a qualified BMW specialist is highly recommended to identify potential issues before purchase. Pay close attention to the following common problems and recalls:

### Known Problems

| Issue | What To Know/Check |
|---|---|
| AC System Issues | Common failures include the AC condenser (prone to damage due to exposed location), evaporator, and compressor. Symptoms include warm air from vents, unusual noises, or complete loss of cooling. Inspect for leaks, proper fan operation, and listen for compressor clutch engagement. |
| Crank Hub Failure | While the failure rate is low (estimated around 0.5%), a crank hub spin is a catastrophic engine failure. Symptoms can include a sudden loss of power or engine noise. For cars that are tracked or heavily modified, preventative measures (e.g., upgraded crank hub capture plate or full crank hub replacement) are often considered. During a PPI, check for any signs of previous engine work or modifications related to the crank hub. |
| Oil Cooler Location | The oil cooler is located in a vulnerable position behind the front bumper, making it susceptible to damage from road debris. Inspect the oil cooler for any signs of impact damage, leaks, or bent fins. Aftermarket guards are available to provide additional protection. |
| Rocks between Dust Plate and Brake Rotor | Small stones or debris can occasionally get lodged between the brake dust shield and the rotor, causing a loud grinding or scraping noise. This is usually not serious and can often be resolved by carefully removing the debris or driving in reverse and braking gently. |
| DCT Issues | Symptoms include slow engagement from 1st to Reverse, sluggish downshifts from 2nd to 1st, or general harshness. Often related to mechatronic unit failure or clutch wear. A software update may resolve some issues, but hardware replacement can be costly. Test thoroughly during a PPI, paying attention to low-speed maneuvers and shifts. |
| Minor Cosmetic Issues | Peeling or cracking of interior trim, particularly around the door handles, center console, and steering wheel buttons. Inspect these areas for wear and tear. |
| Carbon Buildup | As with most direct-injection engines, carbon buildup on intake valves can occur over time, leading to reduced performance and efficiency. While the S55 is less prone than some, it's still a consideration. Walnut blasting or chemical cleaning may be required at higher mileages. Look for rough idle or hesitation under acceleration. |
| Burning Leg Problem | Some owners report hot air blowing from the driver's side footwell vent, often attributed to a faulty blend door actuator or HVAC control unit. Check for consistent temperature output from all vents and proper function of temperature controls. |
| Wheel Balance Issues | Vibrations at highway speeds can be caused by improperly balanced wheels, bent rims, or worn suspension components (e.g., control arm bushings). Ensure wheels are road-force balanced and inspect suspension for play. |
| Fuel Injector Defect (NHTSA Recall 20V-666) | Affects certain 2020 M2 Coupe and 2020 M4 Coupe models. Fuel injectors installed without a damping component can lead to fuel leaks and fire risk. Verify this recall has been addressed by VIN. |
| TSB SI B34 06 15: Grinding Noise from Rear Brakes | Affects early 2015-2016 F80/F82/F83 models with standard compound brakes. Caused by foreign materials getting trapped between the brake dust shield and rotor. BMW issued updated protection plates to mitigate this. Inspect for these updated plates. |
| Fuel Tank Leaks | Reports of fuel tank leaks, particularly around the fuel pump flange or fuel lines. Inspect the underside of the vehicle for any signs of fuel stains or odors, especially after refueling. |
| Rear Axle Noise (New Brakes) | A turbine-like noise from the rear axle during deceleration can occur in the first 300 miles with new brakes. This is generally considered normal during the bedding-in process and should subside. |
| Electrical System Issues | Common issues include parasitic battery drain, often linked to faulty modules (e.g., comfort access, telematics) or improper coding. Check battery health, charging system, and ensure all electronic accessories power down correctly when the car is off. |
| Front Suspension Noises | Cracking or clicking noises from the front suspension when turning the steering wheel, especially at low speeds or over bumps. Often caused by worn strut mounts, control arm bushings, or sway bar end links. Inspect these components for wear. |
| Brake Squeal/Honk | Functional noises (squealing or honking) when braking shortly before coming to a stop are common with performance brake pads, especially carbon ceramic brakes. This is often normal but can also indicate worn pads or rotors. Listen for excessive or persistent noise. |
| Rear Wheel Arch Noise | A "cracking" sound from the rear wheel arch area on smooth or uneven roads can indicate issues with the rear subframe bushings or body panel flex. Inspect the subframe mounting points and surrounding bodywork. |
