# Porsche GT3 991.2 (2018–2019) Buyer Guide

## 1. Introduction

The 991.2 GT3 is the version Porsche got definitively right: a 4.0-liter naturally aspirated flat-six producing 500 hp, a proper six-speed manual transmission offered at no charge, rear-axle steering, and a chassis tuned on the same production line as the Cup race car. It depreciated sharply off its $147,810 launch price and now sits in a range where the performance-per-dollar ratio is genuinely difficult to match. The hack logic is straightforward — buy a used GT3 at a fraction of new cost and own one of the best analog sports cars ever built. The main risk is track use: these cars attract enthusiast buyers who push them hard, and a PPI that misses oil leaks, suspension wear, or any engine anomaly is an expensive mistake.

## 6. Common Problems / Recalls

PPI reminder: Use a Porsche specialist with GT car experience. Focus on engine oil consumption, suspension wear, and any evidence of hard track use — not generic used-car wear.

### Known Problems / Inspection Issues

| Issue | What To Know / Check |
|---|---|
| Engine failures (isolated) | A small number of 991.2 GT3s experienced engine failures potentially linked to supplier component issues. Verify service history for any engine-related work; ask for documentation of any warranty repairs. |
| Oil leaks from oil tank pipe | Reported on some examples. Inspect the oil tank and associated piping during PPI. Cost varies by severity. |
| Exhaust valve tubing coming undone | Minor issue reported by some owners — tubing can detach from exhaust valve actuator. Relatively inexpensive repair but worth checking. |
| Paint quality | More paint complaints reported on 991.2 GT3 vs. prior generation. Inspect paint carefully under proper lighting; check for orange peel, chips, and any resprayed panels. |
| Rear suspension creaking (TSB 142/17) | Creaking noise from rear chassis caused by coil spring force outside tolerance. TSB remedy involves replacing coil springs, shock absorbers, supporting mounts, and inserts. Verify if TSB has been performed. |
| Track use wear | These cars are frequently tracked. Check brake rotor condition, tire wear patterns, and suspension components for signs of hard use. |

## 8. Conclusion

The 991.2 GT3 is a strong hack candidate when bought with the manual transmission, front lift, and Sport Chrono. It is one of the last pure naturally aspirated GT cars Porsche built before the platform shifted, and that makes it a genuine collector piece at current used prices. The main risk is buying a car with undisclosed track damage or an engine issue that a non-specialist PPI missed. Weak-spec PDK cars with no lift and full bucket seats are harder to move and should be bought only at a meaningful discount.
